How to Spot a Bootleg Amiibo Before You Buy

Why this matters more than it used to

As certain amiibo (especially older, discontinued ones) have climbed in resale price, counterfeit and bootleg NFC figures have become more common on general marketplaces. They're rarely a problem when buying from a major retailer's own listing, but get riskier on third-party marketplace sellers and secondhand listings.

Checks before you buy

  • Check the seller, not just the listing. A listing sold and shipped directly by a large, established retailer carries far less risk than a small third-party seller with few reviews.
  • Compare the box printing. Bootlegs often have slightly off color saturation, blurrier text, or a font that doesn't quite match official packaging — search official product photos for comparison before buying secondhand.
  • Look at the base underside. Official amiibo bases have consistent, clean molding and a printed model number; bootlegs frequently have rougher plastic seams.
  • Be suspicious of "too good" pricing on rare figures. If a genuinely scarce, discontinued amiibo is priced well under going resale rate, that's the biggest red flag of all.

What happens if you tap a bootleg on your Switch?

Some bootlegs simply won't register at all since they use incompatible or improperly formatted NFC chips. Nintendo has also released system updates in the past aimed at blocking unofficial NFC toys from functioning, so even a bootleg that works today isn't guaranteed to keep working.

The safest rule of thumb

Buy new releases and common figures from major retailers directly, and save the extra scrutiny for rare, discontinued, or unusually cheap secondhand listings.
