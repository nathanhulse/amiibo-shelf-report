The History of Amiibo: From 2014 Launch to Today's Collector Market

A Wii U-era idea that outlived the Wii U

Amiibo launched in late 2014 alongside Super Smash Bros. for Wii U, initially framed as a way to bring AI-controlled "fighters" trained on your amiibo's data into battles. The NFC figure concept wasn't entirely new — Skylanders and Disney Infinity had already proven toys-to-life could sell — but Nintendo's take leaned harder into collectibility than gameplay depth.

Early scarcity built the collector culture

Certain first-wave amiibo (Marth, Wii Fit Trainer, and a few others) were produced in limited numbers and became notoriously hard to find at retail, which pushed resale prices up fast. That early scarcity — more than any single game feature — is arguably what turned amiibo from "neat accessory" into "thing people actively hunt and collect."

Expansion beyond Smash

Over the following years, support spread across Nintendo's lineup: Animal Crossing, Splatoon, Zelda, Mario Kart, Kirby, Fire Emblem, Xenoblade, and many others each added their own amiibo lines and their own (usually modest) in-game bonuses.

Where the market stands now

Most current-release amiibo are easy to find at normal retail price. The collector premium is concentrated in older, discontinued figures — particularly first-wave Smash figures and short-run special editions — which is why doing a quick price-history check before buying an older figure secondhand is worth the extra minute.

Why they've stuck around

Unlike a lot of toys-to-life competitors that faded within a couple of years, amiibo have outlasted the console generation they launched on because Nintendo kept the line low-stakes: figures are decorative first, functional second, which makes them easy to collect purely for the shelf appeal even if you never touch the in-game bonus.
