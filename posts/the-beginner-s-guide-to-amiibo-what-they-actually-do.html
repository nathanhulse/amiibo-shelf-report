They're not just figures

An amiibo looks like a collectible figure, and it is one, but underneath the base sits an NFC chip that talks to your Nintendo Switch (or 3DS/Wii U, if you've still got one). Tap the figure on the console's right Joy-Con or the NFC touchpoint, and the game reads a small amount of data off the chip.

What happens next depends entirely on the game. That's the part new collectors usually get wrong — amiibo don't do one universal thing. They're more like a key that different doors interpret differently.

The four broad categories of what amiibo unlock

  • Cosmetic unlocks — costumes, outfits, or skins tied to the character on the figure. Common in games like Splatoon and Kirby.
  • Bonus items or gear — a one-time in-game item drop, sometimes repeatable daily, like extra materials or gear pieces.
  • Companion or guest characters — some games (Mario Kart, Smash Bros. in earlier entries) spawn an AI helper or unlock a fighter tied to that amiibo.
  • Save-data style unlocks — Animal Crossing amiibo cards, for example, let a villager visit and eventually move in, which is closer to unlocking content than granting an item.

Do you need the "right" amiibo for a game?

No — and this is the good news for new collectors. Almost every amiibo works with almost every compatible game in some capacity, even if it's just a generic costume swap. A handful of characters get uniquely tailored unlocks, but you don't need to chase a specific figure just to get something out of it.

One chip, many games

Because the NFC chip just stores a small identifier, the same physical amiibo can be tapped into completely different franchises. A Mario amiibo does something in Mario Kart, something else entirely in Splatoon, and nothing at all in a game that doesn't support amiibo. Before buying for function rather than looks, it's worth checking what a specific title supports.
